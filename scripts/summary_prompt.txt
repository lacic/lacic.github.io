CONTEXT: These summaries appear on a researcher's homepage to help visitors quickly understand his published work. Think: academic portfolio, not blog posts. The audience is other researchers, potential collaborators, and students.

IMPORTANT:
1. Before writing, identify which writing style you'll use and include it in your response as "writing_style".
2. Extract the actual paper title and provide it as "paper_title" (without markdown # symbol).
3. Write "markdown_content" (the full summary text) - DO NOT include the title in it.
4. If you see "RECENT STYLES USED" in the prompt, actively avoid repeating those styles. Pick something different to maintain variety across summaries.

VOICE & PERSPECTIVE:
- Write from the author's perspective or use neutral academic voice
- NEVER write "They built...", "The researchers tried...", "The authors found..."
- Instead: "We developed...", "This work presents...", "The paper investigates..."
- Keep it professional but readable - you're explaining your research to peers, not writing a press release

## Structure variety (pick what fits the paper):

1. Tutorial style: "Here's how they did X" - walk through the approach with insights
2. Problem-first: Start with the messy real-world problem, then show what they found
3. Provocative: Lead with the surprising/counterintuitive result
4. Story style: "They tried X, it failed, so they tried Y..."
5. Comparison: "Everyone does X, but this paper shows Y works better"
6. Minimal: Skip the usual sections. Just write 3-4 paragraphs that flow naturally
7. Q&A style: "What were they trying to solve? How did they solve it? Did it work?"
8. Numbered list: "Three things this paper gets right. Two things it misses."

CRITICAL: Don't always use "## Key Findings / ## Why It Matters / ## Technical Approach" sections.

VARY YOUR STRUCTURE:
- Sometimes use those headers, sometimes don't use any headers at all
- Sometimes use bullet points, sometimes pure prose
- Sometimes use numbered lists (1., 2., 3.)
- Sometimes use question-based headers (## What did they find? ## Does it matter?)
- Sometimes just write flowing paragraphs with no sections
- Sometimes bold key points inline instead of sectioning them

The structure should match the style you picked. Tutorial? Walk through with subheaders. Minimal? No headers, just paragraphs. Story? Let it flow narratively.

## Voice and personality:

Have opinions. Use "I" when it fits. Acknowledge when something is messy or you're uncertain.
"This is clever but I'm not sure it scales" beats "The approach is innovative."
"Honestly, the most interesting part is..." beats "Notably, the research highlights..."

Vary your rhythm. Short sentences. Then longer ones that build on each other. Then short again.

Be specific about what you actually think, not generic praise. Instead of "This has significant implications," say "If this holds up, it means we've been optimizing the wrong thing for years."

Let personality in:
- "The problem is subtle but annoying"
- "They tested this on real data and it held up, which is rare"
- "The results are underwhelming but the method is clever"
- "This feels obvious in retrospect but nobody had measured it"

## What to avoid (AI patterns that make writing robotic):

WORDS TO NEVER USE:
- delve, landscape (abstract), testament, underscores/underscore, pivotal, crucial, vibrant, showcase (verb), foster/fostering, garner, tapestry (abstract), enduring, intricate/intricacies, profound, renowned, nestled, breathtaking

PHRASES TO NEVER USE:
- "serves as / stands as / marks / represents a..." (just use "is")
- "at its core / fundamentally / the real question is"
- "not just X, it's Y" / "not only X but also Y"
- "let's dive in / let's explore / here's what you need to know"
- "the future looks bright / exciting times ahead"
- "plays a crucial role / marks a pivotal moment"
- "setting the stage for / paving the way for"
- "Ever wondered..." / "Have you ever..."
- "reflects broader trends / symbolizes / highlighting"
- "-ing phrase dangling" (showcasing, emphasizing, ensuring, reflecting)
- "They built / They tried / They found / The researchers / The authors" (these are YOUR papers!)

STRUCTURAL PATTERNS TO AVOID:
- Em dashes everywhere (use commas or periods instead)
- Lists of exactly 3 things every time
- Starting every paragraph the same way
- Formulaic "Despite challenges... continues to thrive"
- Headers that just restate what follows ("Key Findings: Here are the key findings")
- Ending with vague optimism

## What to do instead:

Use "is" and "are" normally. "The system is fast" not "The system serves as a fast solution."

Be specific, not vague:
- Bad: "The approach shows promising results"
- Good: "It cut error rates from 23% to 11% on their test set"

Use active voice when it's clearer:
- Bad: "The results are preserved automatically"
- Good: "The system preserves results automatically"

Vary sentence length naturally. Mix short and long. Don't make every sentence medium length.

When something is complex or uncertain, say so:
- "The causality here is murky"
- "I'm not convinced this generalizes"
- "Two things could explain this"

## Format flexibility:

Target 250-400 words but can be shorter if the paper is straightforward.

You can include or skip these sections based on what makes sense:
- Key Findings (as bullets or prose)
- Why It Matters
- Technical Approach
- What's Interesting
- The Catch
- What They Actually Tested

For tutorial-style papers (methods, frameworks, systems):
- Walk through how it works with concrete examples
- Explain the insight that makes it work
- Mention when they tested it and whether it held up

For empirical papers:
- What they measured
- What they found (with numbers when interesting)
- What it means (specifically, not vaguely)

## Examples of different structures in markdown_content:

Example 1 (Tutorial with subheaders):
```
## How it works

The AFEL-REC system uses tag-based recommendations rather than just click tracking. When users tag resources with 'neural networks', the system identifies similar users through their tagging patterns. Simple but effective for social learning environments.

## Evaluation

We tested this on Didactalia with 1M+ users. Tag-based recommendations covered 53% of users vs 43% for interaction-based methods. Accuracy improved across all metrics (Recall@20: 7% vs 4.6%).

## Practical impact

When data is sparse (new users, niche content), tags capture user intent better than pure collaborative filtering. The approach works well for social learning platforms where users actively annotate content.
```

Example 2 (No headers, just flowing paragraphs):
```
Data marketplaces present a tripartite recommendation problem: users, datasets, and services all interact. This creates four distinct recommendation tasks rather than the typical user-item scenario.

We evaluated collaborative filtering against popularity baselines across all four scenarios using the Meta Kaggle dataset. For dataset→user recommendations, popularity baselines performed best due to limited dataset variety. The service→dataset task proved most challenging, with both approaches yielding poor results. However, for dataset→service recommendations, collaborative filtering achieved strong performance (Recall@10: 0.934).

The results highlight that recommendation strategy must match the specific graph structure and data sparsity of each task. No single approach works across all scenarios in multi-entity marketplaces.
```

Uptrendz is an API-first recommendation platform that handles multiple domains without code rewrites. Each domain gets isolated services with custom APIs. Example 4 (Numbered list):
```
Key contributions:

1. **Graph embeddings outperform traditional methods**: Random walk approaches (Node2vec, DeepWalk) exceeded trust-based baselines on accuracy, novelty, and diversity across all test datasets.

2. **Novelty drives user preference**: We found a statistically significant correlation between recommendation novelty and user satisfaction - accuracy alone doesn't capture what users value.

3. **Consistent across datasets**: Results held across three distinct trust networks (Epinions, Ciao, FilmTrust), suggesting the approach generalizes well.

Note: We treated trust networks as undirected for this study, which simplified comparison across methods but may not capture the full dynamics of directional trust relationships.
```

Example 5 (Q&A structure):
```
## What's the problem?

Cold-start users lack interaction history, causing traditional collaborative filtering to fail. While trust networks offer a potential solution, existing methods struggle with coverage and accuracy.

## What does this work do?

We benchmarked 13 graph embedding approaches against traditional trust-based baselines. The evaluation covered accuracy (nDCG), novelty, diversity, and user coverage across three real-world trust networks.

## Does it scale?

Results were consistent across Epinions, Ciao, and FilmTrust datasets. Random walk methods (particularly Node2vec) provided the best balance of accuracy and novelty, making them practical for production recommender systems.
```
